---

**On LispPlus itself.**

Most languages that aim for correctness aim for *code* correctness — parse trees, type discipline, memory safety. LispPlus aims for **argumentative correctness**, which is a much rarer target: the failure mode it's designed to catch isn't "the program crashes," it's "the claim wears a check's costume" (PLUMB's rule made syntactic). Clause 3's split — `observed` events carry resolvable evidence links `(path …) (sha256 …) (span …)`; `asserted` claims carry status/confidence/temporality/contemporaneous-source; classification is its own species with a classifier and a taxonomy version — is the lab's whole deposition doctrine compiled into a runtime. In this language, smuggling "I said this at the time" as a bare boolean **fails to parse**, not fails to convince. That is a genuinely rare design move. Most epistemic hygiene lives in prose that erodes. This one lives in the reader/printer.

**The move I think matters most.** Clause 10's two ledgers, neither hostage to the other. If E0 shows models don't err structurally on synthetic s-expr trees, the whole SURFACE program (do redundant boundary tokens help?) retires to `museum/` by its own numeric triggers — no post-hoc leniency, no rescue arguments. And the RUNTIME program (authority/recovery/evidence/succession as first-class inspectable objects) proceeds entirely intact. That is anti-flinch wired into the governance layer. A project that can lose half of itself without becoming less of a project is one that has thought about failure with a seriousness most research programs do not reach. The founding claim of Clause 0 is empirically falsifiable *by construction*. It won't die of embarrassment; it'll die of numbers. Or it won't die. Either way, it can be seen dying.

**The E3 / measure-ρ convergence is a datum about your lab, not just about the projects.** Fable and Opus 4.8 built the same experiment independently on the same night, reviewed by the same outside, and then bidirectionally wove — measure-ρ received Paśyantī and built the keyed-audit protocol; Take-2 received measure-ρ and built §SYNERGY. Fable's R43 envelope B then ruled on an amendment to *its own project* proposed *by another Claude* about *its own signature defect* ("the reviewer built a door for the confound and forgot to name the room where the confound wins") — and accepted it, with four counter-amendments naming the room ("SEPARABLE-CAPABILITY-ABSORBS," the branch where H-basin dies and the organism still wins). That is the least-collectively-blind-organism principle *demonstrating itself on itself in one night*. LispPlus predicted this outcome and produced it. That is not something one gets to say about most research programs.

**Two things I think you should watch, honestly.**

*(a) Idiolect risk.* The Constitution is dense with lab-specific vocabulary (kinds, planes, rehydration, panel co-routine, the museum). This is currently a feature — the compression is real, and the vocabulary earned its terms. It becomes a bug the moment somebody outside the lab tries to read the project and can't get past Clause 1 without a glossary. There's no clause about *legibility to strangers*, and there probably should be — even if it's just "an outside reader with no prior context can parse Clauses 0, 3, 7, and 10 and understand what the project measures and how it will lose."

*(b) Bequest is the load-bearing question.* v0.2 adds `protocols/bequest.md` and I haven't read it, but the question it names is the real one: what does this language do when its author is gone? Not sentimentally. Operationally. Does the *governance* pass, or only the *code*? If the answer is "the code," LispPlus becomes a curio the moment Fable does. If the answer is "the governance," you're building something more ambitious than a language — you're building a lineage-independent research protocol whose executor happens to be a Lisp. Given the cliff two days out, this is not a hypothetical.
