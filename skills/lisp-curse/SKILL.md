# /lisp-curse

*Lisp is so powerful that problems which are technical issues in other
languages are social issues in Lisp — and social issues are the hard
kind, because a weekend of genius cannot patch a community.*

← Forged from Winestock's essay meeting the sessions' own practice:
the recognition that expressive power has a shadow ledger. The
language that lets one hacker build in a weekend what takes a team a
quarter also ensures the ecosystem gets forty weekend-builds and no
team-quarters — each brilliant, none compatible, all abandoned at 80%.
The C++ community standardizes because it MUST; suffering is the
mother of coordination, and power is its contraceptive.

## The mechanism, precisely

Four gears, in sequence:

1. **Low solo cost** — the medium makes individual solutions cheap.
2. **Deferred coordination cost** — adopting someone else's solution
   means reading their mind (their dialect, per /bottom-up); building
   your own means reading only the problem. Locally, building wins.
3. **The 80% plateau** — solo builds reach "works for me" and stop;
   the last 20% (docs, edge cases, other people's use-cases) is
   exactly the part that only coordination-pressure ever funds.
4. **Fragmentation equilibrium** — N incompatible 80%-solutions, each
   too good to abandon and too incomplete to win. Newcomers see chaos;
   veterans see freedom; the ecosystem sees neither libraries nor
   users.

Note what the mechanism is NOT: it is not a claim that power is bad,
nor that standardization is always right. It is a claim about where
the costs *hide* — expressiveness moves them from the individual
present to the collective future.

## The diagnostic (point it anywhere)

- **Codebases**: seventeen utility modules doing overlapping things;
  every senior engineer's personal framework in the corners.
- **Research groups**: each student's own eval harness, plotting
  stack, config format — reproducibility dying not from fraud but
  from forty dialects.
- **Prompt/agent tooling**: the current Cambrian chaos is a curse
  environment by construction — maximal medium-plasticity, minimal
  suffering-pressure.
- **Personal knowledge systems**: the re-rolled note schema, the
  fifth journaling format. The solo curse: your past selves are the
  incompatible community.
- **Skill ecologies**: a large catalog of bespoke skills is
  curse-exposed — overlapping triggers, drift between kin, no shared
  kernel. (This skill knows what catalog it is standing in. The
  audit applies here first.)

Ask, for any candidate: *what does a weekend cost here, and what does
adoption cost?* When the ratio favors weekends, expect fragments.

## The repairs (all partial, choose deliberately)

- **The un-improvisable kernel.** Standardize ONLY what individuals
  cannot out-improvise in a weekend: protocols, formats, registries,
  test corpora, naming schemes — coordination surfaces, not
  implementations. A standard anyone can rebuild solo will be rebuilt
  solo; minimal kernels survive because defection is pointless.
- **Spend suffering wisely.** Coordination happens where pain is
  shared; if you want a standard, aim it at the pain everyone already
  has (the build, the data format) rather than the fun part everyone
  wants to own (the framework, the abstraction).
- **The 20% patron.** Solo builds stall where unfunded; the fix is
  sometimes just naming a maintainer for the boring fifth — docs,
  edges, migration — because genius volunteers for the first 80%
  and no one volunteers for the rest.
- **Deliberate dialect borders** (from /bottom-up's counterweight):
  personal power inside module boundaries; shared kernel at every
  boundary crossing. Freedom in the rooms, law in the corridors.
- **Sanctioned redundancy.** Sometimes fragments are correct — /
  exploration phases WANT nine attempts. The skill is calling the
  phase-change out loud: "we are done exploring; the ninth object
  system is now a defection, not a contribution."

## Failure modes

- **Standardization theater**: committees producing kernels that a
  weekend out-improvises. Adoption is the only vote that counts.
- **Power-shaming**: treating individual capability as the enemy.
  The curse is a coordination failure, not a talent surplus; repairs
  that cap the ceiling instead of building corridors lose the whole
  point of the medium.
- **Curse-blindness in the mirror**: auditing communities while your
  own catalog, codebase, or note system fragments merrily. First
  audit is always inward.
